What is carbon monoxide poisoning?

Carbon monoxide (CO) poisoning occurs when individuals inhale a dangerous amount of this odorless, colorless gas. According to health agencies like the Centers for Disease Control and Prevention, the risk is particularly acute for those who are asleep or under the influence, as they may succumb to the gas before experiencing typical symptoms such as headaches, dizziness, or memory loss.

Relatives believe that 31-year-old Felecia Richey and her sons, Tysin, 13, and Bentley, 10, likely succumbed to carbon monoxide poisoning.

The Mayo Clinic elaborates that when CO accumulates in your bloodstream, it can lead to fatal or severe tissue damage. This is because red blood cells, which usually carry oxygen, end up transporting carbon monoxide instead when CO levels are high, particularly in confined spaces.

It’s crucial to note that CO is generated when burning fuels like gas, wood, propane, or charcoal. Poorly ventilated appliances and engines can allow this gas to build up to lethal levels.

If exposed, immediate access to fresh air is essential, followed by urgent medical attention.
